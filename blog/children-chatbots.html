Children and AI Chatbots: Weighing Concerns About Regulation

September 27, 2025

AI chatbots are now able to engage in natural, emotional conversations with people. They can also convincingly simulate the personalities of specific people and characters. It has been reported that growing numbers of children and teenagers are spending significant time interacting with these chatbots, and there is anecdotal evidence that some are developing what appear to be meaningful relationships with them.

Some groups have raised concerns about these interactions. They worry that if children form relationships with chatbots, they will fail to form social relationships, experience stunted emotional growth, and be vulnerable to behavioural manipulation by chatbot providers. They are calling for governments to intervene to protect children from these potential harms.

These concerns are well-founded and, as a result, it would be overall beneficial for governments to place restrictions on the ability of children to access AI chatbots.

I'm ~70% confident the concerns about harms are valid, but only ~30% confident that government restrictions would be beneficial overall. Granted, I've limited knowledge of how AI impacts children. I know AI chatbots can be addicting and emotionally manipulative.

But, I also want to be careful about assuming that chatbot relationships cause social problems. It might be that socially struggling kids gravitate toward AI chatbots as safer alternatives and might already be socially isolated. I'm hesitant about AI chatbot regulation because chatbots operate globally while regulations are national. Restrictive policies in one country could push children to black-market or unregulated platforms. I also don't know how to distinguish harmful chatbots from beneficial ones, such as tutors or mental health support systems. Aggressive content monitoring or age verification could even be more privacy-invasive than the harms they try to prevent.

I think regulation is needed, but AI evolves so fast that policy could quickly become obsolete or counterproductive. Longitudinal studies comparing children who do and don't use chatbots intensively should be conducted to measure the most effective policy approaches (e.g., parental controls, usage limits, or age verification).
