Two Critical Failure Modes of AI Agents in Export Control Monitoring

November 1, 2025

If the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) deployed AI agents to continuously monitor shipping manifests, customs declarations, corporate filings, and other documents to detect export control violations, the system could fail badly or create new problems in distinct ways.

Failure 1: Adversarial Prompt Tampering

Adversaries could tamper with AI prompts by embedding adversarial content that causes the AI to misclassify. For example, shell companies could include specific linguistic patterns, formatting quirks, or metadata signatures that the agent learns to associate with “legitimate” transactions during training. If agents autonomously investigate and clear transactions, bad actors could inject prompts in shipping documents or customs forms that cause agents to misinterpret their instructions.

This matters because it creates an adversarial arms race where adversaries constantly look for weaknesses in the AI. Some ways to mitigate this include continuously red-teaming agents with synthetic evasion attempts before deployment. If an agent encounters document patterns outside its training distribution or confidence intervals, mandatory human review should be triggered.

Failure 2: Diffused Accountability

If BIS becomes structurally dependent on AI and lacks human expertise to validate agent outputs—or technical capacity to intervene when agents fail—it risks losing its ability to audit performance or recognize systematic errors. When AI agents make mistakes, accountability becomes unclear: is BIS responsible, or the AI developer?

For instance, if an agent autonomously clears a transaction that later proves to be a major violation (like the TSMC–Huawei case), who bears responsibility? This is dangerous because BIS may institutionalize unverified automation, eroding oversight integrity.

To mitigate this, AI agents should produce human-readable audit trails showing reasoning chains for every decision. Additionally, humans should be required to approve any transaction exceeding $10 million or involving entities on export watchlists.
